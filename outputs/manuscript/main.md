# DEGORA: auditable prioritization from heterogeneous published DEG tables

## Abstract

Public transcriptomic studies often provide differential-expression gene (DEG) tables rather than uniformly reprocessable expression matrices with complete phenotype labels, effect variances, or standard errors. Existing transcriptomic meta-analysis software covers several important regimes, including p-value combination, rank aggregation, effect-size meta-analysis, raw-expression or GEO-centered workflows, and curated signature resources. DEGORA (a public agora for differentially expressed gene evidence) addresses a narrower but common use case: building a source-auditable gene-evidence database from heterogeneous as-published RNA-seq and microarray DEG summaries. It harmonizes DEG rows, collapses related contrasts within independent source units, applies predeclared source-quality weights, ranks genes by repeated, directionally consistent support, and exports both gene scores and browser-inspectable evidence records. In human positive-control benchmarks built from only 2-17 independent source units per topic, the fixed quality-weighted DEGORA ranking recovered canonical marker panels at recall@100 of 0.93 (interferon response), 0.73 (heat shock), and 0.85 (hypoxia/HIF) on RNA-seq evidence, and 0.78 for ER stress/unfolded protein response on the combined RNA-seq plus microarray corpus (0.59 on RNA-seq alone). These recall values matched or modestly exceeded standard summary-level meta-analysis methods rather than dominating them: on two of the four topics (IFN and hypoxia) the best non-DEGORA comparator tied DEGORA at recall@100 and on the other two (ER stress and heat shock) DEGORA's point estimate modestly exceeded the best comparator within overlapping intervals, so the benchmark establishes faithful recovery of known biology, not statistical superiority. The locked panels were expanded to 26-33 canonical genes per topic (the heat-shock panel additionally excludes HSPA5, an ER/ATF6-branch chaperone rather than an HSF1 target), and recall@100 held between 0.60 and 0.92 on larger 40-50 gene supplementary panels, arguing against a small-panel artifact. Adding human microarray evidence raised IFN recall@20 from 0.43 to 0.61 and ER-stress recall@10/50/100 from 0.22/0.50/0.59 to 0.25/0.59/0.78, while IFN recall@50 dipped from 0.82 to 0.79 and hypoxia recall stayed 0.85 at every cutoff with marker-order shifts. DEGORA is therefore not a universal replacement for raw-expression or variance-aware meta-analysis; it provides an auditable integration and local database layer for the public-summary regime where RNA-seq and microarray DEG evidence must be interpreted together.

## Introduction

Transcriptomic meta-analysis is methodologically strongest when each study contributes comparable sample-level expression data, phenotype annotations, or effect-size variances. This setting is served by established approaches including p-value combination for RNA-seq studies, rank aggregation, random-effects or effect-size frameworks, and web, package, or atlas workflows that retrieve, normalize, or uniformly reprocess expression matrices (Rau et al., 2014; Kolde et al., 2012; Haynes et al., 2017; Toro-Dominguez et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Sokolowski et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2016). However, retrospective evidence synthesis often starts from a less complete input: supplementary DEG tables generated by different analysis pipelines, platforms, and reporting conventions. Such tables may provide gene identifiers, log2 fold-changes, adjusted p-values, and ranks, but often omit the expression matrices, sample-level labels, standard errors, or package-specific fields required by some formal meta-analysis workflows.

DEGORA is designed for this public-summary regime. The method does not claim that only DEGORA can compute a numerical rank after RNA-seq and microarray DEG summaries are harmonized. Fisher, Stouffer, AWFisher, metaRNASeq, RobustRankAggreg, MetaVolcanoR p-combination, and rank-product approximations can also operate on summary-compatible p-value or rank representations when those representations are available (Li and Tseng, 2011; Rau et al., 2014; Kolde et al., 2012; Prada et al., 2019). The distinction is the output contract. DEGORA treats the ranked list as one layer of an evidence database: each score is accompanied by source-unit support, direction concordance, source-quality weighting, descriptive heterogeneity, platform/probe metadata, and per-source evidence rows.

Related resources already occupy parts of this space, and DEGORA does not claim to be the first to make differential-expression evidence browsable. Curated, source-attributed DEG atlases and knowledge bases exist, including DEET and the disease-specific dbMDEGA and MiceDEGdb resources, and large efforts now mine gene sets directly from the supplementary tables of publications (Sokolowski et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2017; Podkolodnaya et al., 2025; Clarke et al., 2024). Quality-weighted prioritization of genes by repeated support across many heterogeneous published lists has also been demonstrated, most directly by MAIC, which learns a per-source information-content weight and returns source-traceable per-gene evidence (Parkinson et al., 2020). DEGORA is therefore not novel in any single one of these ingredients; its contribution is their specific combination for the as-published DEG-table regime: ingesting heterogeneous RNA-seq and microarray DEG summaries rather than re-processed count matrices, collapsing contrasts so that one paper counts as one source unit, ranking genes by predeclared and auditable source-quality weights together with direction concordance rather than learned or optimization-derived weights, surfacing descriptive heterogeneity, and distributing the result as a fully offline, self-contained evidence database. Relative to MAIC specifically, DEGORA's weights are predeclared and inspectable rather than information-content-derived, its organizing axis is direction concordance with explicit heterogeneity flags, and its evidence layer ships as a static offline artifact rather than a hosted service.

This distinction matters for resource construction. A NutriOmics-style database, for example, may need to answer whether a nutrient exposure repeatedly increases or decreases a gene across published RNA-seq and microarray experiments. In that setting, the practical question is not only whether a combined p-value can be computed, but whether the result remains inspectable: which source units contributed, whether the direction was consistent, whether a microarray row came from a full limma table or a normalized-matrix fallback, and whether one paper with multiple contrasts has been prevented from counting as multiple independent studies. DEGORA was built to make those evidence records first-class outputs rather than post hoc annotations.

## Results

### DEGORA uses a fixed quality-weighted ranking

The manuscript-facing analyses use `degora_quality_weighted_score` as the fixed DEGORA ranking wherever it is available. This source-quality-weighted lane is implemented in the codebase as a secondary variant of the primary `degora_score`; it is predeclared as the single manuscript-facing DEGORA row so that no DEGORA score is selected per topic after the gold panels are locked. The unweighted `degora_score` and `degora_slice` outputs remain in the result files as reference indices, but Table 1 and Figure 2 do not select the best DEGORA row per topic. This design prevents benchmark-row selection against the locked gold panels.

The quality-weighted score is a prioritization index rather than a calibrated probability. It combines source-unit support, source-quality support, direction confidence, evidence strength, and leave-one-source-unit-out stability while preserving the underlying rows. Current score outputs also report descriptive source-unit heterogeneity fields (`heterogeneity_q`, `heterogeneity_df`, `heterogeneity_i2`), a categorical `heterogeneity_flag`, a heterogeneity-aware RE-Stouffer reporting lane, a beta order-statistic RRA rho lane, a source-unit beta-binomial direction posterior, and an auxiliary random-effects log2FC reporting lane. These fields are exposed for review and sensitivity analysis rather than folded into the locked DEGORA score.

High heterogeneity was common and should be interpreted as an evidence-review signal. The current score tables flagged 2,960/9,860 IFN genes, 10,448/14,239 ER-stress genes, 5,854/14,285 heat-shock genes, and 17,880/32,687 hypoxia genes as `high_context_dependent_review`. These labels did not remove genes from the ranking; they mark cases where the Atlas evidence view should be inspected before treating a gene as a context-stable marker.

### Human positive-control benchmarks recover canonical response genes

Four human benchmark topics were used as positive controls: type I interferon response, ER stress/unfolded protein response, heat shock, and hypoxia/HIF. These axes were selected because their transcriptional programs have well-established marker biology: type I interferons induce interferon-stimulated genes through JAK-STAT/ISGF3 signaling; ER stress activates the unfolded protein response through PERK, IRE1, and ATF6 branches; heat shock is coordinated by heat shock factors and molecular chaperones; and hypoxia is mediated by HIF transcription factors that regulate angiogenesis, glycolysis, pH regulation, and oxygen-homeostasis genes (Ivashkiv and Donlin, 2014; Schoggins, 2014; Walter and Ron, 2011; Akerfelt et al., 2010; Semenza, 2012).

In Table 1 and Figure 2, fixed quality-weighted DEGORA recovered canonical markers at useful early ranks. On RNA-seq evidence, IFN achieved recall@10/50/100 of 0.29/0.82/0.93, heat shock 0.31/0.58/0.73, and hypoxia 0.21/0.76/0.85. The ER-stress row in Table 1 is computed on the combined RNA-seq plus microarray corpus (its input mode is labeled explicitly in Table 1) and achieved 0.25/0.59/0.78; on RNA-seq-only evidence ER stress reached recall@10/50/100 of 0.22/0.50/0.59, where DEGORA modestly exceeded the best non-DEGORA comparator (0.59 vs 0.56; Table 2). Exact 95% recall intervals at recall@100 remained wide even for the expanded locked panels (26-33 genes): IFN 0.76-0.99, ER stress 0.60-0.91 (combined corpus), heat shock 0.52-0.88, and hypoxia 0.68-0.95. Top-100 hypergeometric enrichment FDR values were small across all four topics (all < 1e-35), but the runnable comparators reached similarly small enrichment FDRs at the same cutoff, so these values confirm that the rankings are enriched for known biology rather than distinguishing methods. The top-ranked genes were biologically coherent: IFN prioritized `RSAD2`, `CMPK2`, `MX1`, `TAP1`, `IFIT1`, and `IFIT3`; ER stress prioritized `DDIT3`, `HSPA5`, `HERPUD1`, `TRIB3`, and `PDIA4`; heat shock prioritized `HSPA6`, `HSPA1A`, `HSPA1B`, `DNAJB1`, and `BAG3`; and hypoxia prioritized `EGLN3`, `NDRG1`, `ANGPTL4`, `PFKFB4`, `TMEM45A`, `HK2`, `ADM`, and `VEGFA`.

The comparator results support a faithfulness interpretation rather than a superiority claim. At recall@100, DEGORA tied the best runnable non-DEGORA comparator on IFN (0.93 vs 0.93) and hypoxia (0.85 vs 0.85); on heat shock its point estimate exceeded every comparator (0.73 vs 0.58), although the 26-gene panel leaves the confidence intervals overlapping; and on ER stress it exceeded the best comparator on both the combined RNA-seq plus microarray corpus (0.78 vs 0.75) and the RNA-seq-only corpus (0.59 vs 0.56; Table 2), again within overlapping intervals. DEGORA showed modest early- and mid-rank gains on IFN (recall@10 0.29 vs 0.18; recall@50 0.82 vs 0.79) and heat shock (recall@10 0.31 vs 0.23; recall@50 0.58 vs 0.54), whereas on hypoxia an untuned Stouffer baseline exceeded it at recall@10 and recall@50 (0.27/0.82 vs 0.21/0.76). The benchmark therefore shows that DEGORA recovers established marker biology as well as standard summary-level methods while adding an auditable evidence layer, not that it is a uniformly superior statistical test.

These recall values remain locked-panel summaries rather than population-level performance estimates. IFN, ER stress, and heat shock are useful positive controls because their marker biology is well established, but they are thin public-summary corpora: IFN used 2 RNA-seq source units in the primary table and 3 after microarray addition, ER stress used 3 human RNA-seq source units (4 after microarray addition), heat-shock genes were supported by 2-3 source units, and hypoxia--the deepest corpus, and the one where DEGORA's advantage over standard methods was smallest--used 15 RNA-seq source units (17 after microarray addition). Exact intervals and source-unit bootstrap summaries are therefore reported as uncertainty guards, not as proof that the benchmark panels estimate all possible marker sets. To test whether the recovery estimates were an artifact of small panels, each locked panel was expanded to 26-33 canonical genes (IFN 28, ER stress 32, heat shock 26, hypoxia 33; the heat-shock panel dropped HSPA5 because it is an ER/ATF6-branch chaperone rather than an HSF1 target), and recall@100 held between 0.60 and 0.92 on still-larger 40-50 gene supplementary panels (IFN 0.92, hypoxia 0.85, ER stress 0.72, heat shock 0.60), so the recovery signal is not a small-panel artifact.

### RNA-seq and microarray evidence can be integrated without hiding platform provenance

Figure 3 and Table 2 evaluate whether adding human microarray DEG evidence changes marker recovery when the same quality-weighted DEGORA ranking is used. IFN remained strong after adding microarray evidence: recall@100 stayed 0.93, recall@50 shifted from 0.82 to 0.79, and recall@20 increased from 0.43 to 0.61. The mixed IFN top ranks included canonical interferon-stimulated genes such as `CMPK2`, `RSAD2`, `IFI44L`, `IFIT1`, `OASL`, `IFI27`, `OAS2`, `OAS3`, and `IFI6`. ER stress showed the clearest and most consistent gain: adding microarray evidence raised recall at every cutoff, with recall@10 from 0.22 to 0.25, recall@50 from 0.50 to 0.59, and recall@100 from 0.59 to 0.78. Hypoxia remained stable at recall@100=0.85, with recall unchanged at every cutoff and changes only in marker ordering.

These results justify a narrow cross-platform claim. Microarray addition did not uniformly improve every topic or cutoff, and the manuscript should not imply that it did. Instead, the data show that DEGORA can merge RNA-seq and microarray DEG evidence while retaining the metadata needed for audit: assay type, platform, source unit, contrast count, source-quality score, probe-collapse or normalized-matrix derivation notes, and direction consistency. In the current comparator set, several p-value/rank methods could also compute a mixed-platform rank after harmonization. DEGORA's advantage is that the mixed rank is produced together with a structured evidence database that makes the cross-platform support inspectable.

### Comparator coverage separates runnable same-input methods from different-input workflows

The benchmark included summary-compatible comparator families that can faithfully consume the current public DEG evidence representation: Fisher and Stouffer p-value combination, AWFisher, metaRNASeq Fisher, metaRNASeq inverse-normal where informative, RobustRankAggreg, MetaVolcanoR p-combination, sign voting, and an explicit rank-product approximation. These methods provide a fair same-input comparison because they use gene-level p-values, signed z-scores, or ranks reconstructed from the same harmonized DEG rows.

Other tools were treated differently because their required inputs were not present in the public DEG-only benchmark files. hStouffer is designed for large-scale RNA-seq p-value stabilization and, in its published workflow, uses raw count data from ARCHS4 and DESeq2-derived outputs; the current public tables do not provide complete original DESeq2-like fields for all source units (Kim et al., 2026). AWmeta/AW-REM and effect-size modes require per-study variances or standard errors. DEGORA now emits an explicitly labeled derived-SE random-effects log2FC reporting lane for review, but this is not treated as a faithful substitute for running external variance-aware tools that require original variance inputs (Yang et al., 2025). RankProd exact, MetaDE effect-size modes, DExMA, MetaIntegrator, crossmeta, ImaGEO, NetworkAnalyst, OMiCC, DEET, and CREEDS are therefore represented as blocked, covered, orthogonal, or resource-prior-art rows depending on their actual input requirements and output scope (Haynes et al., 2017; Toro-Dominguez et al., 2019; Toro-Dominguez et al., 2022; Pickering, 2024; Zhou et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Sokolowski et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2016).

This separation is important for reviewer interpretation. A blocked row is not a DEGORA win. It records that the tool addresses a different data regime or requires inputs absent from as-published DEG summary tables. Conversely, where a comparator can run faithfully from p-values or ranks, it is included and reported even when it matches or exceeds DEGORA at a cutoff.

The same boundary applies to tuning. Comparator rows use package defaults or explicitly documented public-summary adapters, and the current benchmark does not include a symmetric hyperparameter search for every baseline. The manuscript therefore treats the comparison as a faithful same-input default benchmark, not as evidence that DEGORA dominates tuned implementations of all comparator families.

### The Atlas turns the ranking into a local evidence resource

Figure 4 summarizes the local DEGORA Atlas package. The current generated Atlas contains six corpus configurations spanning the four biological topics (IFN RNA-seq 9,860 and RNA-seq plus microarray 12,777; ER stress RNA-seq plus microarray 14,239; heat shock 14,285; hypoxia RNA-seq 32,687 and RNA-seq plus microarray 34,785), totaling 118,633 scored gene rows; because IFN and hypoxia each contribute both an RNA-seq-only and a combined-platform configuration, gene rows are not deduplicated across configurations of the same topic. The Atlas also carries 114 method/result rows and 23 prior-art/resource rows. It includes a static HTML dashboard, SQLite database, source-data workbook, and figure panels. The dashboard supports gene search, corpus filtering, direction filtering, method recall heatmaps, source-support summaries, and gene detail panels. Corpus-level `degora_scores.db` files retain detailed source evidence, allowing a user to inspect why a gene ranked highly and which source units support or contradict that signal.

To make this auditability directly usable, the resource also ships the DEGORA Evidence Explorer: a single self-contained HTML file that embeds the SQLite evidence database together with a WebAssembly build of SQLite (sql.js) and executes real queries entirely in the browser. It requires no server, no network, and no installation, so it runs from a local file or an archived release and remains functional in long-term repositories. Self-contained, server-free HTML explorers for differential-expression results have been described before, including DrEdGE and Rvisdiff (Tintori et al., 2020; Barrios et al., 2024); the delivery mechanism is therefore not itself new, and DEGORA's distinction is that it ships a curated, multi-source evidence database queried by full client-side SQL over relational provenance records rather than a fixed-view dashboard of a single dataset. For any gene the Explorer resolves the full per-source provenance behind its rank---each contributing source unit with its assay type, platform, cell system, analysis pipeline, replicate counts, direction, signed-z, normalized rank, and a hyperlink to the originating GEO record---alongside the source-quality weighting, sign concordance, heterogeneity flag, and random-effects effect size with its 95% confidence interval. It supports direction-aware and corpus-level filtering, cross-corpus rank comparison for one gene, gene-level permalinks, and per-gene and per-query CSV export. This is the inspection layer that a ranking-only method does not provide: a user can see not only that a gene is prioritized but exactly which heterogeneous published studies, on which platforms and in which directions, support or contradict that call. Supplementary Table 8 contrasts the Explorer's provenance, interactivity, and offline-deployment features with public DEG and gene-evidence portals (DEET, CREEDS/RummaGEO, ARCHS4, Enrichr/SigCom LINCS, and Open Targets). The embedded provenance covers the top-ranked genes of every corpus; the complete per-source evidence for all scored genes remains in the corpus-level `degora_scores.db` files shipped with the resource.

This resource output is central to the paper's use case. DEGORA is intended to help researchers build topic-specific evidence databases from public DEG files. The ranked list is useful, but the database view is what makes the result reviewable: users can inspect direction, source count, reliability, heterogeneity flag, and the original evidence rows behind a gene.

## Discussion

The current results support DEGORA as an auditable public-DEG evidence framework rather than a replacement for all transcriptomic meta-analysis methods. When public files contain enough summary information, p-value and rank-based comparator methods can compute mixed RNA-seq/microarray rankings. DEGORA's contribution is that it makes this operating regime explicit and returns database-ready evidence objects instead of only a combined statistic. This is valuable when the scientific question is whether a gene is repeatedly and directionally associated with a perturbation across heterogeneous published studies.

The benchmark is best read as a faithfulness check rather than a demonstration of superiority. On two of the four topics (IFN and hypoxia) every runnable summary-level method, including DEGORA, converged to the same recall@100, so IFN in particular is a ceiling-effect positive control. On the other two topics DEGORA's recall@100 point estimate exceeded the best comparator, most clearly on heat shock (0.73 versus 0.58) and more modestly on ER stress (0.78 versus 0.75), although the locked panels leave the confidence intervals overlapping. Microarray addition improved IFN early recall at rank 20 and ER-stress recall at every cutoff, with a slight IFN recall@50 decrease. Hypoxia is a useful counterweight: DEGORA recovered credible HIF-axis genes, but an untuned Stouffer baseline exceeded it at early cutoffs, ruling out any universal superiority claim. The consistent finding across topics is that DEGORA loses nothing against standard methods while adding an auditable, source-resolved evidence layer.

Several limitations remain. First, DEGORA scores are prioritization indices, not calibrated posterior probabilities or formal meta-analysis p-values. The auxiliary RE-Stouffer, RRA, and random-effects log2FC fields are reporting lanes for sensitivity review, not replacements for the locked score or for native raw-expression meta-analysis. Second, normalized-matrix microarray fallback contrasts are lower-confidence than author-provided full DEG tables; in the current GSE19519 ER-stress source, the Welch fallback does not model paired or family structure because the necessary design metadata are unavailable in the derived public matrix. Third, tools requiring raw expression, phenotype labels, original DESeq2 columns, or per-study variance estimates cannot be fairly run from DEG-only files; a separate raw-expression benchmark would be needed to compare against those methods on their native input regime. Fourth, although source-unit collapse reduces independence inflation from multiple contrasts within one paper, it cannot remove publication bias, study-selection bias, or biological context dependence. This is visible in the high heterogeneity-flag rate for ER stress and hypoxia, including 17,880/32,687 hypoxia genes. Finally, the locked gold panels used for Tables 1 and 2 consist entirely of up-regulated canonical markers, so direction-aware recall is numerically identical to plain recall in every row reported there. To probe the direction-assignment machinery directly, we additionally assembled a locked 16-gene hypoxia-repressed panel spanning mitochondrial-biogenesis, oxidative-phosphorylation, mismatch-repair, homologous-recombination, and MYC-target genes and scored it against the hypoxia RNA-seq corpus. DEGORA assigned the curated down direction to 15 of the 16 repressed markers (the single exception, PPARGC1A, is flagged in the panel as context-invertible), showing that the direction-confidence and beta-binomial posterior components are functional rather than vacuous annotations. However, none of the 16 repressed markers ranked within the top 100 (recall@100 = 0; the best-ranked repressed gene, ODC1, appeared at rank 187 of 32,687), confirming that DEGORA's evidence-strength prioritization in a stimulus-response corpus is dominated by the strongly and repeatedly induced canonical program. Down-regulated marker recovery, as distinct from correct direction labeling, therefore remains limited and is not claimed; recovering repressed markers would likely require a corpus enriched for the repressed contrast or a direction-stratified ranking. Finally, DEGORA is deliberately scoped as a transparent integration-and-audit layer rather than a learned statistical pipeline: it does not auto-detect each table's source pipeline (assay and pipeline labels are taken from the user-supplied catalog rather than inferred by a classifier), its high-confidence flag uses predeclared thresholds rather than a formal triplet gate that combines BH-adjusted magnitude, rank, and sign p-values, its source weighting is a predeclared square-root-of-sample-size and source-quality heuristic rather than a learned multi-feature model, and it operates at the gene level without hierarchical gene-to-pathway propagation or empirical-Bayes shrinkage. Automated pipeline classification, a probabilistic triplet-concordance gate, learned quality weights, and pathway-level integration are natural future extensions but are not required for, and are not claimed by, the present resource. These limitations are exposed through source notes, feasibility matrices, heterogeneity flags, exact recall intervals, null-enrichment summaries, and the Atlas evidence view rather than hidden behind a single score.

The practical implication is that DEGORA fits a resource-building niche. For a user constructing a nutrient-response, cytokine-response, stress-response, or perturbation-response database from public supplements, the goal is often a transparent, inspectable ordering of genes by repeated directional support. In that setting, DEGORA provides a reproducible bridge from heterogeneous DEG tables to a local evidence atlas while keeping comparator boundaries explicit.

## Methods

### Input data and harmonization

Each active DEG source is converted to a long-format evidence table with gene symbol, log2 fold-change, p-value or signed-z surrogate, adjusted p-value when available, within-study normalized rank, assay type, platform, source path, source URL, and curation notes. Full author-provided DEG tables are preferred. Processed-matrix microarray fallbacks are accepted only when explicitly labeled, and their derivation method is carried into source notes and quality metadata.

### Source units and contrast collapse

`paper_id` is used as the independent source unit when present; otherwise `study_id` is used. Related time points, contrasts, or probes from one source unit are collapsed before consensus ranking so that one paper cannot count as multiple independent studies. Probe-level microarray rows are collapsed to gene symbols before within-study rank calculation. Source-unit consensus avoids selecting the most extreme contrast as the sole representative; it instead aggregates evidence within the source unit before cross-source scoring.

### Quality-weighted score

The manuscript-facing ranking is the fixed `degora_quality_weighted_score`. It combines source-unit support, source reliability, direction confidence, rank evidence, signed-z evidence, effect evidence where available, and leave-one-source-unit-out stability. Source weights are predeclared from source metadata and capped to prevent large processed matrices or multi-contrast papers from dominating independent-source evidence. The score is reported as a relative prioritization index and top-percent rank, not as a calibrated probability. The individual ingredients of this ranking are established rather than new: direction-aware vote-counting and rank aggregation across studies (Kolde et al., 2012; Prada et al., 2019; Fischer et al., 2022), study- or sample-quality weighting in expression meta-analysis (Siangphoe et al., 2019), and quality-weighted repeated-support gene prioritization across heterogeneous published lists (Parkinson et al., 2020). DEGORA does not claim a new combination statistic; its choices are to make the weights predeclared and auditable and to attach every score to an inspectable per-source evidence record.

### Heterogeneity and evidence audit

For each scored gene, DEGORA reports descriptive heterogeneity over collapsed source-unit signed-z values (`heterogeneity_q`, `heterogeneity_df`, `heterogeneity_i2`) and a categorical `heterogeneity_flag`. It also emits a heterogeneity-aware RE-Stouffer z reporting lane computed as the fixed-effect Stouffer z divided by `sqrt(1 + I2 * (k - 1))`, where `k` is the number of contributing source units. These fields are used for audit and review, not as formal random-effects inference. Per-gene evidence tables preserve contributing source units, assay type, platform, source-quality label, direction, log2 fold-change, p-value, normalized rank, and source notes.

### Auxiliary statistical reporting lanes

DEGORA adds several post-score statistical summaries without changing the locked score. Direction confidence is reported as a source-unit beta-binomial posterior mean, `(1 + concordant source units) / (2 + observed source units)`, with a separate reliability-weighted variant. Rank evidence is also summarized with a RobustRankAggreg-style beta order-statistic rho over source-unit normalized ranks using the declared source-unit universe. Effect evidence is summarized with a random-effects inverse-variance log2FC lane using standard errors derived from `abs(log2FC) / abs(signed_z)` when exact standard errors are unavailable. The SE-derived effect lane is labeled as approximate: the relation SE = abs(log2FC)/abs(signed_z) recovers the true standard error only for Wald-type statistics (for example DESeq2 or edgeR Wald tests) and is biased for moderated-t (limma) or likelihood-ratio statistics. It is most interpretable in larger-k corpora such as hypoxia.

### Comparator classification

Comparator methods were classified by whether they could faithfully consume the current public DEG summary inputs. Summary-compatible methods were executed where possible: Fisher, weighted and unweighted Stouffer, AWFisher, metaRNASeq Fisher, informative metaRNASeq inverse-normal, RobustRankAggreg, MetaVolcanoR p-combination, sign vote, and rank-product approximation. Comparator runs used package defaults or documented summary-compatible adapters, not exhaustive tuned configurations. Methods requiring unavailable raw expression matrices, phenotype/sample metadata, original DESeq2-specific fields, replicate-origin labels, or per-study variances were emitted as blocked or different-input rows with explicit failure modes. Blocked rows were not counted as DEGORA wins.

### Microarray handling

Microarray evidence enters through full limma-style DEG tables when available or through explicitly labeled normalized-matrix fallback derivations. Probe identifiers are mapped to gene symbols and collapsed before scoring. All four benchmark topics are restricted to human sources by an explicit species gate applied before scoring; for ER stress this gate excludes the mouse sources GSE84450 and GSE103667, leaving three independent human RNA-seq source units (GSE102505, GSE296996, and GSE245918, all tunicamycin versus vehicle) as the RNA-seq-only ER baseline. In the current cross-platform benchmarks, IFN uses GSE71634, ER stress uses GSE19519 tunicamycin and thapsigargin contrasts from one source unit (a large lymphoblastoid donor panel, so its p-values are driven by effect size rather than significance and its added evidence differs in cell system from the RNA-seq sources), and hypoxia uses GSE3045 and GSE22282. Because the added microarray sources differ in cell system and, for IFN, in interferon subtype from the RNA-seq sources, the cross-platform recall gains are confounded with biological context and should be read as evidence that platform integration is feasible without loss rather than as a controlled platform comparison. All derived microarray and count-derived contrasts in these benchmarks use per-gene Welch t-tests on log-transformed values rather than limma empirical-Bayes or negative-binomial models, and the normalized-matrix fallbacks do not model paired or family structure when that design cannot be reconstructed from available public metadata.

### Benchmark metrics

Gold-panel recovery was summarized by recall@10, recall@20, recall@50, and recall@100 against locked marker panels. Each recall is accompanied by an exact binomial confidence interval, top-k precision, and a hypergeometric enrichment p-value with Benjamini-Hochberg correction across methods at the same cutoff. Background-negative AUROC and average precision use the locked positive panel versus all other ranked genes as background negatives, avoiding the degenerate three-housekeeping-gene ROC setting. Direction-aware recall was computed where expected directions were defined; because all locked gold panels used for Tables 1 and 2 contain only up-regulated markers, direction-aware recall equals plain recall by construction in every row reported there. As a dedicated direction-validation check outside Tables 1 and 2, a locked 16-gene hypoxia-repressed panel (`data/studies/gold/hypoxia_hif1_repressed_panel.csv`, all markers curated as down-regulated with cited evidence) was scored against the hypoxia RNA-seq corpus; DEGORA's consensus-direction call matched the curated down direction for 15 of 16 markers while none of the repressed markers reached the top 100, so the direction-assignment component is independently validated even though repressed-marker recovery is not claimed (see Discussion). This comparator summary is provided as `hypoxia_hif1_repressed_comparator_summary.csv`. DEGORA was not selected per topic by benchmark performance; the fixed quality-weighted rank was used for all manuscript-facing DEGORA rows. Tables and figures were regenerated from the comparator summaries and cross-platform benchmark summary files listed in the source sidecars.

The DEGORA package, the benchmark and figure-generation scripts, the harmonized DEG tables, the locked gold panels, and the manuscript-facing comparator summaries are released under the MIT License (see `LICENSE`); curated data are released under CC-BY with attribution to the source studies and GEO accessions. Manuscript tables are generated under `outputs/tables/manuscript/`, figure packages under `outputs/figures/manuscript/`, and corpus-level score databases under `outputs/results/*/degora_scores.db`; the complete set of display items is regenerated with `make -C outputs/code paper`.

The reference implementation, command-line tool, and generated database files retain the original `degora` prefix (for example `degora_scores.db` and the `degora` command); these are stable identifiers for the software package and are not re-labeled here. Before journal submission the repository and curated corpus will be archived with a permanent DOI (Zenodo) and the reference list will be completed with dataset-level citations for every GEO accession; these remaining packaging items are listed in the submission checklist below and do not affect any analysis reported here.
